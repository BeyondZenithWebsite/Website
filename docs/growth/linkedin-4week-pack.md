## Week 1

### Post 1 (POV)
Most delivery delays in 10–100 person teams are not talent issues.

They’re operating-system issues:
- unclear decision rights
- too many parallel priorities
- no shared definition of “done”

If your roadmap keeps slipping, start with cadence and ownership before tooling.

What’s the biggest delivery bottleneck in your team right now?

### Post 2 (Story)
A leadership team came in saying, “Engineering is too slow.”

Baseline reality:
- priorities changed weekly
- dependencies were invisible
- incidents kept resetting sprint plans

We didn’t start with a rewrite.
We started with weekly governance, risk visibility, and explicit trade-offs.

Within weeks, decision latency dropped and delivery confidence improved.

The lesson: speed follows clarity.

### Post 3 (Framework)
A simple 30–90 day delivery recovery structure:

1) Days 1–14: baseline and bottlenecks
2) Days 15–45: sequence high-leverage interventions
3) Days 46–90: lock operating cadence and handover

If you want, I can share the checklist we use for this.

### Post 4 (Contrarian)
Hot take: hiring more engineers is often the most expensive way to fix delivery.

If your system is misaligned, you just scale confusion.

Fix decision quality first.
Then scale capacity.

Agree or disagree?

## Week 2

### Post 5 (POV)
AI strategy fails when it starts with tools instead of outcomes.

Start here:
- one business bottleneck
- one measurable metric
- one accountable owner

Everything else is theatre.

### Post 6 (Story)
We reviewed an “AI roadmap” with 14 initiatives.

The team shipped none.

Why?
- no prioritisation logic
- no data readiness baseline
- no implementation owner

We cut to 3 use-cases and got movement.

Constraint creates momentum.

### Post 7 (Framework)
AI use-case prioritisation formula we use:

Impact × feasibility × risk-adjusted time-to-value.

If a use-case can’t score across all three, it’s not “phase 1”.

Want the scoring template?

### Post 8 (Proof)
Common pattern in growth-stage teams:
- too many “important” initiatives
- not enough operational clarity

When priorities and decision rights are explicit, execution improves fast.

This sounds simple, but most teams skip it.

## Week 3

### Post 9 (POV)
“Technical debt” is too vague to manage.

Translate it into business risk:
- release delay risk
- incident risk
- cost growth risk

If it doesn’t map to risk, it won’t get prioritised.

### Post 10 (Story)
A founder asked for a full platform rebuild.

We ran due diligence first.
Result: 20% of the proposed rewrite addressed 80% of business pain.

The rest was expensive anxiety.

### Post 11 (Framework)
Before any major rewrite, ask:
1) What must change for business outcomes?
2) What can be stabilised instead of rebuilt?
3) What is the rollback plan?

If you can’t answer #3, don’t start.

### Post 12 (Contrarian)
Another hot take:

“Move fast” without governance is just delayed rework.

Execution discipline is not bureaucracy.
It’s velocity insurance.

## Week 4

### Post 13 (POV)
CTO advisory should be measured by business outcomes, not deck quality.

Good advisory changes:
- decision speed
- delivery predictability
- risk profile

### Post 14 (Story)
When teams move from ad-hoc decisions to weekly governance, leadership stress drops.

Not because work disappears.
Because uncertainty gets managed.

### Post 15 (Framework)
Board-ready tech update in 4 blocks:
- delivery predictability
- platform risk
- AI initiative status
- next 30-day decisions needed

Simple beats comprehensive.

### Post 16 (CTA/proof)
If your team is 10–100 people and delivery confidence is slipping, that’s fixable.

A focused 30–90 day intervention is usually enough to reset trajectory.

Happy to share what “good” looks like.
